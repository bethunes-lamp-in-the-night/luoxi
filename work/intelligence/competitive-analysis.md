## CASE 1: Lei Yang Death (雷洋案, 2016, China)

### Background
Environmental scientist Lei Yang died in police custody in Beijing (May 2016). Family used social media to demand investigation, faced censorship and pressure.

### What Worked
1. **Social media documentation** - Wife posted timeline and evidence on Weibo
2. **Professional credibility** - Lei was middle-class professional (Renmin University graduate), relatable victim
3. **Legal persistence** - Family hired lawyers, used legal channels while also using social media
4. **Public outrage** - Middle-class fear ("could be me") drove engagement
5. **International attention** - English-language media covered extensively
6. **Sustained pressure** - Family didn't give up despite censorship

### What Failed
1. **Censorship ultimately won** - Story eventually suppressed, discussion contained
2. **No systemic change** - Officers received minimal punishment, no police reform
3. **Family fatigued** - Years of pressure took toll, advocacy eventually quieted
4. **No viral symbol** - Unlike Luoxi, no single powerful image/fact that persisted
5. **Political framing** - Became too political (police state narrative), easier to censor
6. **Limited celebrity support** - Few public figures risked speaking out

### Lessons for Luoxi Campaign

**DO:**
- ✅ Use legal channels WHILE doing social media (dual track)
- ✅ Emphasize "ordinary family" relatability
- ✅ Pursue international media coverage early
- ✅ Document everything in real-time
- ✅ Frame as universal human issue, not just political

**DON'T:**
- ❌ Rely only on domestic platforms (get international presence early)
- ❌ Let it become solely about political system (medical ethics more defensible)
- ❌ Expect quick resolution (prepare for long campaign)
- ❌ Assume outrage alone creates change (need specific demands, pressure points)

**Key Takeaway:** **Persistence matters, but censorship eventually wins without international infrastructure and depoliticized framing.**

## CASE 2: Vaccines and Related Deaths (山东疫苗案, 2016, China)

### Background
Scandal over improperly stored vaccines being sold across China. Multiple children affected, parents organized online, government investigation forced.

### What Worked
1. **Multiple victims = critical mass** - Not single case, pattern was undeniable
2. **Parent community formation** - Parents connected, shared stories, organized
3. **Documentary evidence** - Medical records, vaccine batch numbers, traceable
4. **Government forced to respond** - Scale of problem couldn't be ignored
5. **International implications** - WHO, international health community engaged
6. **Regulatory changes resulted** - New vaccine management laws passed
7. **Celebrity support** - Some public figures spoke out for vaccine safety

### What Failed
1. **Censorship of parent groups** - WeChat groups shut down, organizers harassed
2. **Incomplete accountability** - Few prosecutions relative to scale
3. **Continued distrust** - Scandal damaged vaccine confidence, public health impact
4. **Parent exhaustion** - Many families gave up after years
5. **Economic settlement focus** - Government offered money, many accepted, reduced pressure

### Lessons for Luoxi Campaign

**DO:**
- ✅ Document pattern if other victims exist (strength in numbers)
- ✅ Engage international health/medical community early
- ✅ Pursue systemic reform (laws, regulations), not just individual accountability
- ✅ Build parent community infrastructure (mutual support)
- ✅ Maintain moral high ground (reject money-for-silence)

**DON'T:**
- ❌ Let compensation offers divide movement
- ❌ Rely solely on domestic organizing platforms (backup international channels)
- ❌ Accept partial justice as full victory
- ❌ Ignore long-term public health implications

**Key Takeaway:** **Scale matters (multiple victims > single case) but sustaining movement after partial government response is critical challenge.**

## CASE 3: Charlie Gard Case (2017, UK)

### Background
British infant Charlie Gard had rare genetic disorder. Parents fought hospital/courts for experimental treatment in US. International viral campaign, ultimately unsuccessful (Charlie died).

### What Worked
1. **Parental sympathy maximum** - "Parents fighting for child's life" universal story
2. **International viral spread** - Pope, Trump, celebrities weighed in
3. **Simple narrative** - "Let parents try to save their baby"
4. **Crowdfunding success** - £1.3 million raised in days
5. **Media saturation** - Every major outlet covered
6. **Legal battle visibility** - Court cases gave structure, updates, drama
7. **Photo/video content** - Baby's images, parents' pleas, emotional power

### What Failed
1. **Outcome didn't change** - Despite global attention, Charlie died
2. **Backlash emerged** - Some saw parents as prolonging suffering
3. **Medical community divided** - Experts questioned experimental treatment
4. **Fatigue set in** - Months-long saga exhausted sympathies
5. **No systemic change** - No policy reforms, just one family's tragedy

### Lessons for Luoxi Campaign

**DO:**
- ✅ Parental voice centered (maximum sympathy)
- ✅ Simple, emotional narrative (not too complex)
- ✅ Visual content (photos, videos) central
- ✅ International engagement (Pope/celebrity equivalent for Luoxi)
- ✅ Crowdfunding if needed (shows grassroots support)

**DON'T:**
- ❌ Let campaign become ONLY about this one family (must have systemic angle)
- ❌ Drag on too long without victories (fatigue risk)
- ❌ Ignore medical expert concerns (need some expert validation)
- ❌ Accept "awareness raised" as only outcome (push for concrete reform)

**Key Takeaway:** **Viral parental campaigns can achieve massive attention but often fail to create systemic change. Must translate sympathy into policy pressure.**

## CASE 4: #MeToo Movement (2017+, Global/China)

### Background
Viral movement against sexual harassment/assault. Started in US, spread to China (#MeToo, #米兔, #WoYeShi 我也是) despite censorship.

### What Worked
1. **Hashtag simplicity** - #MeToo incredibly easy to participate
2. **Personal stories at scale** - Thousands sharing = undeniable pattern
3. **Celebrity amplification** - Famous voices gave permission to others
4. **Low barrier to entry** - Just share your story
5. **Mutual support** - Victims found community, reduced shame
6. **Multiple platforms** - When censored on one, moved to another
7. **International pressure** - Global movement made local censorship harder
8. **Concrete outcomes** - Some perpetrators faced consequences

### What Failed (China-specific)
1. **Heavy censorship** - #MeToo banned, discussions deleted
2. **Activist arrests** - Some organizers detained
3. **Limited institutional change** - Few policy reforms in China
4. **Fatigue and fear** - Some victims harassed, doxxed, sued
5. **Hijacking attempts** - Some false accusations damaged credibility

### Lessons for Luoxi Campaign

**DO:**
- ✅ Create simple, shareable hashtag (#JusticeForLuoxi, #RememberLuoxi)
- ✅ Multi-platform strategy (when censored, shift platforms)
- ✅ International solidarity critical (can't be contained domestically)
- ✅ Community building among supporters (mutual reinforcement)
- ✅ Celebrity voices provide cover for ordinary people
- ✅ Document censorship as part of story (resistance narrative)

**DON'T:**
- ❌ Rely on single platform or single account
- ❌ Assume domestic momentum alone sufficient
- ❌ Ignore international organizing from day one
- ❌ Let perfect be enemy of good (small victories count)

**Key Takeaway:** **Censorship can slow but not stop movements with international infrastructure, simple participation mechanisms, and celebrity amplification.**

## CASE 5: Alfie Evans Case (2018, UK)

### Background
Similar to Charlie Gard - British toddler with degenerative neurological condition. Parents fought hospital over treatment withdrawal. Pope, Poland, Italy offered help.

### What Worked
1. **International support infrastructure** - Italy granted citizenship, offered treatment
2. **Religious community mobilization** - Catholic Church support provided organizing base
3. **Clear villain framing** - Hospital as uncaring bureaucracy vs. loving parents
4. **Social media savvy** - Parents used Facebook effectively
5. **Legal battle structure** - Court cases provided narrative milestones
6. **Sustained media coverage** - Months of attention

### What Failed
1. **Outcome unchanged** - Alfie died, hospital decision upheld
2. **Public opinion divided** - Many sided with doctors ("parents in denial")
3. **Protest fatigue** - Supporters at hospital dwindled over time
4. **No policy change** - No reforms to medical decision-making processes
5. **Family trauma** - Parents faced online abuse, years of grief
6. **Movement ended with child's death** - No lasting campaign

### Lessons for Luoxi Campaign

**DO:**
- ✅ Seek international institutional support (offers from other countries, orgs)
- ✅ Build on existing organized communities (religious, advocacy groups)
- ✅ Use legal process as organizing timeline/structure
- ✅ Maintain social media presence throughout

**DON'T:**
- ❌ Make it only about saving this one child (Luoxi already gone - focus on preventing next)
- ❌ Ignore public opinion concerns (must maintain sympathy, avoid "denying reality")
- ❌ Let movement end when legal case ends (build lasting reform campaign)
- ❌ Expect parent trauma alone to sustain movement (need broader organizing)

**Key Takeaway:** **International institutional support crucial. Movement must outlast individual case to achieve reform. Frame as preventing future tragedies, not just this one.**

## CASE 6: Tianjin Explosion (2015, China)

### Background
Massive industrial explosion in Tianjin killed 173, mostly firefighters. Families used social media for accountability, faced censorship but achieved some results.

### What Worked
1. **Visual impact** - Explosion footage, destruction images unforgettable
2. **Number of victims** - Scale made suppression harder
3. **Firefighter families** - Victims were heroes, politically sensitive to ignore
4. **International coverage** - Foreign media extensively covered
5. **Government forced to respond** - Arrests made, compensation paid
6. **Anniversary remembrance** - Annual memorial posts keep story alive
7. **Some transparency** - Investigation details released (unusual for China)

### What Failed
1. **Censorship of criticism** - Critical analysis deleted, only "mourning" allowed
2. **Limited systemic change** - Industrial safety improvements unclear
3. **Family organizing suppressed** - Collective action attempts shut down
4. **Economic settlement** - Compensation offered, many accepted, reduced pressure
5. **Story faded** - After initial months, international attention gone

### Lessons for Luoxi Campaign

**DO:**
- ✅ Use powerful visual content (Luoxi has: grandmother feeding clothes, unsutured wounds fact, etc.)
- ✅ Connect to other victims if pattern exists (other deaths at hospital?)
- ✅ Frame victims as innocent/sympathetic (baby = ultimate innocent)
- ✅ International media coverage essential and early
- ✅ Anniversary remembrance (November 14 annual memorial)
- ✅ Accept partial transparency as progress, push for more

**DON'T:**
- ❌ Let economic settlement end campaign (Luoxi family already rejecting money - good)
- ❌ Accept "mourning allowed, criticism banned" (must keep accountability pressure)
- ❌ Assume scale alone creates change (need sustained organizing)
- ❌ Let story fade after initial burst (long-term strategy needed)

**Key Takeaway:** **Visual impact + international coverage + family refusal of settlement = maximum pressure. But sustaining beyond initial outrage requires infrastructure.**

## CASE 7: Peng Shuai Tennis Star Case (2021, China)

### Background
Chinese tennis star Peng Shuai accused former vice premier of sexual assault on Weibo. Post deleted, she disappeared, international tennis community mobilized, WTA pulled tournaments from China.

### What Worked
1. **International leverage** - WTA had economic power (tournaments), used it
2. **Celebrity status** - Peng was internationally known, couldn't be easily erased
3. **#WhereIsPengShuai** - Simple, viral hashtag
4. **Sports community solidarity** - Tennis players worldwide amplified
5. **Sustained pressure** - Didn't fade after initial burst
6. **Corporate accountability** - WTA chose ethics over profits (rare, powerful)
7. **Western government engagement** - UN, US, other governments spoke out

### What Failed
1. **Peng's fate unclear** - Appeared in staged videos, unclear if safe/free
2. **No accountability** - Accused official faced no consequences
3. **Censorship total** - Discussion completely banned in China
4. **Peng silenced** - No longer speaks about assault
5. **Limited solidarity** - Other Chinese athletes/celebrities stayed silent

### Lessons for Luoxi Campaign

**DO:**
- ✅ Engage international institutions with leverage (medical associations, WHO, etc.)
- ✅ Simple, viral hashtag (#JusticeForLuoxi, #RememberLuoxi, #LuoxisLaw)
- ✅ Identify leverage points (hospital accreditation, international medical partnerships)
- ✅ Sustained campaign (not just initial burst)
- ✅ Western government/UN engagement if possible
- ✅ Community solidarity (medical professionals, parents, advocates)

**DON'T:**
- ❌ Expect Chinese institutions alone to create accountability
- ❌ Rely on domestic celebrity support (they face huge pressure)
- ❌ Accept staged "resolutions" (must verify genuine accountability)
- ❌ Let censorship make you invisible (international presence essential)

**Key Takeaway:** **International institutions with actual leverage (economic, professional) are critical. Celebrity/profile helps but isn't sufficient. Need entities willing to pay cost of standing up.**

## CASE 8:权健 (Quanjian/Quan Jian) MLM Medical Fraud (2018-2019, China)

### Background
Multi-level marketing company selling fake cancer cures. Child died from forgoing real treatment for Quanjian products. Journalist investigation, viral article, company shut down, executives arrested.

### What Worked
1. **Investigative journalism** - "权健，和它阴影下的中国家庭" went massively viral
2. **Child victim** - 4-year-old girl's death drove outrage
3. **Clear villain** - Company, executives, fraudulent products
4. **Documentary evidence** - Medical records, product claims, financial trails
5. **Celebrity involvement** - Company had celebrity endorsers, exposed
6. **Government action** - Company shut down, arrests made, regulations changed
7. **Sustained coverage** - Story developed over weeks, each revelation new hook

### What Failed
1. **Initial censorship** - Article deleted multiple times before going too viral
2. **Delayed justice** - Child died in 2015, justice came 2019 (4 years)
3. **Other victims ignored** - Focus on one case, many others got less attention
4. **Industry persists** - MLM health fraud still exists, system not fixed

### Lessons for Luoxi Campaign

**DO:**
- ✅ Investigative journalism approach (deep dive, documented evidence)
- ✅ Child victim as moral center (maximum outrage)
- ✅ Expose complicity networks (hospital leadership, investigation conflicts of interest)
- ✅ Document everything (autopsy, medical records, timeline)
- ✅ Pursue concrete outcomes (arrests, shutdown, regulations)
- ✅ Each new revelation as new viral moment

**DON'T:**
- ❌ Expect quick justice (prepare for long campaign)
- ❌ Focus only on this case if pattern exists (build broader pattern)
- ❌ Accept company/institution closure as full victory (push for individual accountability)
- ❌ Ignore systemic issues (hospital oversight, medical incentive structures)

**Key Takeaway:** **Investigative journalism format (evidence-rich, narrative-driven) can overcome censorship if viral enough. Child victim + clear villain + documentary proof = winning formula.**
